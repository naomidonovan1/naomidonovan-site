To be honest, I wished this was not the first peer mentoring group meeting. It seems a little strange to me that I'm almost done with my third year in the program 
and this is when we met for the first time. I do think I just missed the sign up for it when it was happening my first year, but to be fair my mental health and 
overall outlook were quite a bit better than. 

Now, I can say definitively that I am so much less optimistic, burnt out, and overall in worse shape than I have been yet during this PhD. While quals was an 
extremely stressful time and it challenged my discipline and self-esteem, I kinda had to do it and do it well for fear of embarassment. It was an actual 
checkpoint and there were clear steps to pass it.

Third year is a whole different challenge. The challenge of third year has been getting out of bed, going to work, 
continuing to work when it feels like absolutely nothing is working, and pretending that everything I'm doing right now will payoff eventually. My optimism is at 
an all time low and it feels like nothing I'm doing is even productive. To make matters worse, I feel unsupported by the mentorship in my lab and this was just
exacerbated by the peer mentoring group meeting.

First of all, the group was myself, David, and Alex. David and Alex are two of the most scientically optimistic people I know in the program and they have also
landed in what seems to be their dream labs. Alex, another third year, wants to continue a career in academia -- something that fully seems within reach for him 
given his enthusiasm and passion for literally all things science (including the writing and reading requirements as well). David, a second year, has strong 
mentors in his lab and feels very well supported in his project. So this was kinda a hard group to talk to given that I feel oppositely. 

I don't want to continue in academia, my passion and enthusiasm for science is so low, perhaps even negative. And I don't have mentors that excite me or help me 
figure out issues in the lab. Suffice to say, talking to them just made me feel like my problems were just my problems. It was validating to hear that they 
also thought they were problems, but it saddened me that they didn't share in my problems. It made me feel further out on this raft that is doing a PhD. 

Sometimes i think about my choice in a lab and I feel like I chose wrong. It's impossible to know what would've been the better choice. I couldn't join either of 
my first two rotations, but I was burned out by the third that I just didn't want to go through the trouble of finding a fourth rotation. I have learned a lot 
being in this lab and just working on my computational skills, but given the ability of AI agents to write code, it feels like a lot of those skills are now 
rendered useless. Ah that's probably a topic for another blog post. 

While peer mentoring is a valiant effort, I think it can also be another point of direct comparison with others in my program. We all have different, and sometimes, 
overlapping issues, so it can be hard to find some similar ground when it feels like we should be exactly the same. In other words, if I'm the only one having 
a particular issue, does that mean something's wrong? Perhaps if there were others at the peer mentoring group it would have mitigated this feeling, but 
it was a hard day or two following that group meeting.
